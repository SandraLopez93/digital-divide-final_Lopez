The Digital Divide in Latin America

Since the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, children across the region of Latin America have lost nearly 4x more days of schooling compared to children from other regions of the world, according to a recent report by Unicef.

Not all countries within the region, however, were impacted in the same way

Nations resorted to a variety of different learning methods. From listening along to radio transmissions and televised programming, to digital applications on computers and phones, efforts to keep students connected was paramount. Given all of these options, how then, were students falling behind at unprecedented rates? This report will compare the various learning methods introduced to students in Brazil, the wealthiest, and most digitally connected nation, and Bolivia, one of the poorest countries in the region.

How connected were Brazil and Bolivia throughout the COVID-19 pandemic?

Wireless Connectivity

The number of connected households in Brazil however, is not equal across the nation. The most economically marginalized commnunities are the most impacted, such as the North and Northeast region, where 46.7% of households are without intenet connection. In total, 4.1 million students are completely disconnected from wireless connection in Brazil.

Radio schooling

While broadcasted through the airwaves, often times radio education is accompanied with digital worksheets, textbooks and lessons on phone applications such as Whatsapp or other free platforms.

Tele-education

While 46.1% of households in Bolivia have television access, that does not necesarily mean that students used this apparatus to facilitate their learning.

Resources

Alternative learning opportunities inclide additional resources that school systems provided to students. This includes: school connectivity, applications through digital platforms, virtual tutoring, digital learning packets, and repositories of digital content
